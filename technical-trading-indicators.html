RSI — Relative Strength Index

Developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. (1978), RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a scale of 0 to 100.

RSI = 100 − (100 / (1 + RS))
where RS = Average Gain over n periods / Average Loss over n periods
Standard period: n = 14

↑ Overbought Zone (RSI > 70)

Price has risen aggressively. May signal an upcoming pullback or reversal. Buyers are exhausted; sellers may step in.

↓ Oversold Zone (RSI < 30)

Price has fallen sharply. May signal a bounce or reversal. Sellers are exhausted; buyers may step in.

  • Bullish divergence: price makes lower low, RSI makes higher low → reversal up
  • Bearish divergence: price makes higher high, RSI makes lower high → reversal down
  • RSI ~50 = neutral momentum; useful as a trend filter

MACD Histogram

The Moving Average Convergence Divergence (Gerald Appel, 1979) tracks the relationship between two exponential moving averages to identify trend direction, momentum, and potential reversals.

MACD Line = EMA(12) − EMA(26)
Signal Line = EMA(9) of the MACD Line
Histogram = MACD Line − Signal Line

✅ Bullish Signals

  • MACD crosses above signal → buy signal
  • Histogram turns from negative to positive
  • Histogram bars growing taller = increasing momentum
  • Bullish divergence: price lower low, MACD higher low

❌ Bearish Signals

  • MACD crosses below signal → sell signal
  • Histogram turns from positive to negative
  • Histogram bars growing more negative = selling pressure
  • Bearish divergence: price higher high, MACD lower high

ATR — Average True Range

Developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. (1978), ATR measures market volatility by decomposing the entire range of an asset price for a period. It does NOT indicate direction — only how much price is moving.

True Range = max(
  |High − Low|,
  |High − Previous Close|,
  |Low − Previous Close|
)

ATR = Simple Moving Average of True Range over n periods (typically 14)
  • High ATR: large price swings, high volatility — wider stops needed
  • Low ATR: small price moves, consolidation — tighter stops possible
  • Position sizing: Risk $ / ATR = number of units to trade
  • Stop-loss placement: Entry ± (1.5× to 3× ATR) is a common rule
  • ATR expanding after a squeeze often precedes a breakout

VWAP — Volume Weighted Average Price

VWAP is the institutional benchmark for fair value during a trading session. It represents the average price weighted by volume, showing where the most capital changed hands.

VWAP = Σ(Price × Volume) / Σ(Volume)

Calculated cumulatively from the session open. Resets each day for intraday use.

✅ Price Above VWAP

  • Buyers are in control
  • Institutions that bought are in profit
  • Bullish bias — look for long entries on pullbacks to VWAP
  • VWAP acts as dynamic support

❌ Price Below VWAP

  • Sellers are in control
  • Institutions that bought are underwater
  • Bearish bias — look for short entries on rallies to VWAP
  • VWAP acts as dynamic resistance
  • Mean reversion: prices tend to gravitate back toward VWAP
  • Execution benchmark: institutions aim to buy below VWAP, sell above VWAP

Bollinger Bands

Created by John Bollinger (1980s), Bollinger Bands are a volatility envelope placed above and below a moving average. The width dynamically adapts to market volatility using standard deviation.

Middle Band = SMA(20)
Upper Band = SMA(20) + 2 × σ(20)
Lower Band = SMA(20) − 2 × σ(20)

σ = standard deviation of closing prices over the last 20 periods

The Squeeze

  • Bands narrow → low volatility → breakout imminent
  • Represents a coiling market building energy
  • Direction of breakout NOT predicted — combine with other indicators

Band Walks

  • Price hugging the upper band = strong uptrend
  • Price hugging the lower band = strong downtrend
  • Touching the band is NOT a reversal signal in trending markets
  • Mean reversion: In ranging markets, price tends to bounce between bands
  • %B indicator: (Price − Lower) / (Upper − Lower) shows where price sits within bands

Volume POC — Point of Control

The Point of Control (POC) is the price level with the highest traded volume in a Volume Profile. It represents the “fairest” price where the most market participants agreed to transact.

Volume Profile: a histogram of volume traded at each price level
POC: the price level with the tallest volume bar
Value Area (VA): the range containing ~70% of total volume (VA High & VA Low)

🎯 POC as Magnet

  • Price is attracted to POC like a magnet
  • Acts as the strongest support/resistance in the profile
  • Price moving away from POC tends to get pulled back
  • High-volume nodes = strong S/R; low-volume nodes = fast movement

📊 Trading the POC

  • Price at POC: expect consolidation, range-bound trading
  • Price above POC: POC acts as support on pullbacks
  • Price below POC: POC acts as resistance on rallies
  • Breakaway from POC: initiating move, trend beginning

EMA 20 — Exponential Moving Average

The 20-period Exponential Moving Average gives more weight to recent prices than a simple moving average, making it more responsive to new information. It is the most widely-used short-term trend filter.

Multiplier (k) = 2 / (n + 1) = 2 / 21 ≈ 0.0952

EMAtoday = (Close × k) + (EMAyesterday × (1 − k))

The most recent price gets ~9.5% weight; older prices decay exponentially

EMA vs SMA

FeatureEMASMA
WeightingExponential (recent = more)Equal for all periods
ResponsivenessFast — reacts quicklySlow — lags more
WhipsawsMore proneFewer false signals
Best forShort-term tradingLonger-term trend

How to Trade It

  • Price above EMA 20: bullish — use as dynamic support
  • Price below EMA 20: bearish — use as dynamic resistance
  • EMA slope up: uptrend; slope down: downtrend
  • Pullback to EMA: classic trend-continuation entry point

Combining Indicators

No single indicator is reliable alone. The key is confluence — when multiple independent indicators align to tell the same story.

🎯 Trend Identification

EMA 20 for direction + MACD for momentum confirmation

Price > EMA 20 + MACD Hist > 0 = Bullish trend confirmed

⏰ Entry Timing

RSI for overbought/oversold + Bollinger Bands for extremes

RSI < 30 + Price at lower BB = High-probability long entry

💰 Fair Value Check

VWAP + Volume POC for institutional price agreement

Price near VWAP & POC = Fair value; expect range-bound action

📏 Risk Management

ATR for stop-loss distance and position sizing

Stop = Entry − 2×ATR | Size = Risk$ / (2×ATR)

The Confluence Framework

Trend (EMA) → Momentum (MACD) → Timing (RSI + BB) → Validation (VWAP + POC) → Risk (ATR)

Common Pitfalls & Best Practices

01 Never rely on a single indicator. RSI oversold alone is NOT a buy signal — always seek confluence from 2–3 independent indicators.
02 Indicators lag. All indicators are derived from past price data. They confirm trends, they don’t predict them. Use price action as the leading signal.
03 Context matters. RSI at 30 in a strong downtrend can stay oversold for weeks. Bollinger Band touches in a trend are NOT reversal signals.
04 Timeframe alignment. Check indicators on multiple timeframes. A bullish signal on the 5-min chart means nothing if the daily chart is bearish.
05 ATR for stops, always. Fixed-dollar or fixed-percentage stop-losses ignore market volatility. ATR-based stops adapt to conditions automatically.
06 VWAP resets daily. Don’t use yesterday’s VWAP for today. For multi-day analysis, use anchored VWAP from significant price levels.
07 Volume validates everything. Breakouts without volume are suspect. POC and VWAP inherently incorporate volume, making them more reliable than pure price indicators.
08 Don’t over-optimize. Default settings (RSI 14, MACD 12/26/9, BB 20/2, EMA 20) work well. Curve-fitting parameters to past data leads to poor forward performance.

Key Takeaways

  • RSI measures momentum — oversold (<30) and overbought (>70) are your watchpoints
  • MACD Histogram shows trend strength — zero-line crossovers are key signals
  • ATR quantifies volatility — use it for position sizing and stop-losses
  • VWAP reveals institutional fair value — price above = bullish, below = bearish
  • Bollinger Bands show volatility extremes — squeezes precede big moves
  • Volume POC is the price magnet — strongest support/resistance level
  • EMA 20 defines the short-term trend — dynamic support/resistance
  • Confluence is king — 2–3 aligned indicators > any single one
